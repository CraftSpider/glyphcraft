Glyphs are diagrammed in a standardized industrial language. This language is likely fairly complex in its entirety, given the ability to diagram highly advanced Glyph lattices such as the Radiance mantles. However, the basics are fairly simple.

Individual glyphs are spelled like {GLYPH}. To indicate one glyph after another in a chain, one uses a dash like so: [[{IDENTIFY}-{DIRECT}]]. This indicates the output of one glyph feeding another. Glyphs may have multiple inputs and outputs, though only some specific setups are known to have textual representations.

To indicate multiple of one glyph all feeding another, one uses `:N` form. As an example, [[{ALIGN}-{DIFFERENTIATE}|{ALIGN}:4-{DIFFERENTIATE}]].

Sub-2 is a shorthand for a second-order (3D) glyph being offset down the Z-axis from its anchor.
- Conjecture: Super-2 is offset up the Z-axis, Sub-1 to Sub-4 indicate other axis to offset on.

There exists a form of CAD software specifically for Glyphcraft known as GWalk. Its use appears widespread among the technical Glyphcrafting community, and it supports all steps from theoretical designing to actual physical lattice layout creation.
